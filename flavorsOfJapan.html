FLavors Around The World : Featuring Japan

Beyond Sushi
Unveiling the Hidden Flavors of Japanese Regional Cuisine

- Ren Saito

Picture stepping out of the bullet train, not into Tokyo's neon-lit streets, but into a secluded, family-owned restaurant in the mountains of Yamanashi. A steaming bowl of hoto—a thick, chewy noodle floating in a rich, miso-based broth, studded with seasonal vegetables—appears, its heat a comforting hug against the cool mountain air. This is Japan outside the world-famous sushi rolls and steaming bowls of ramen. This is the Japan of kyodo ryori, the regional soul food of Japan, where each dish has a story of local ingredients, tradition, and distinctive people's spirit. For so long, Japanese cuisine has been viewed internationally by a limited set of famous dishes. But under the surface exists a culinary rich tapestry interwoven with different flavors, textures, and histories to discover. Let's take a trip, not to Tokyo's crowded fish markets, but to the secret kitchens and neighborhood restaurants where the real soul of Japanese cuisine resides, uncovering the regional specialties that make Japan a paradise for foodies.

Osaka: The Soul of Okonomiyaki

In the heart of Osaka, where the air hums with the energy of street vendors and the aroma of sizzling batter, lies the soul of okonomiyaki. This isn't your average pancake; it's a savory, customizable masterpiece, a culinary free-for-all where ingredients like cabbage, pork belly, seafood, and cheese mingle in a symphony of flavors. Imagine a griddle sizzling with golden-brown batter, the chef's spatula dancing across its surface, expertly flipping and shaping the pancake.

Osaka style okonomiyaki involves mixing all ingredients into the batter before cooking, creating a dense, satisfying experience. Contrast that with Hiroshima's layered approach, where the ingredients are stacked on top of a thin crepe. I had the privilege of meeting Chef Tanaka, a third-generation okonomiyaki master, who shared his secrets: 'The key is in the batter, the quality of the cabbage, and the soul you put into it.' His tiny, smoke-filled shop, 'Tanaka's Delight,' is a testament to this, with walls adorned with photos of satisfied customers. For an authentic taste of Osaka's soul, venture into Dotonbori, where the streets are lined with okonomiyaki restaurants. Try 'Mizuno' for a classic experience or 'Chibo' for a more modern take.

Yamanashi: Hearty Hoto and Mountain Flavors

As the train winds its way through the majestic peaks of Yamanashi, a craving for something warm and comforting sets in. This is hoto country, where the region's rugged landscape has shaped its cuisine. Hoto, a thick noodle stew, is a culinary hug, a bowl of warmth against the mountain chill. Picture a rustic restaurant, its wooden beams darkened with age, filled with the aroma of simmering miso and vegetables. The thick, flat noodles, made from locally grown wheat, absorb the rich broth, infused with pumpkin, mushrooms, and hearty meat. The ingredients are simple, but the flavors are profound, reflecting the region's connection to the land.

I visited a local farm, where they grow the giant kabocha pumpkins that are essential to a good hoto, and the owner explained how the cold mountain nights made the pumpkins extra sweet. The scenery is breathtaking, with views of Mt. Fuji in the distance. Try 'Kosaku' for a traditional hoto experience, or a local farm restaurant for an authentic meal..

Okinawa: Tropical Flavors and Island Influences

In the tropical paradise of Okinawa, where the turquoise waters meet lush green hills, the cuisine is a vibrant reflection of the island's unique culture and climate. Goya champuru, a stir-fry with bitter melon, tofu, and pork, is a testament to this, a dish that balances the bitterness of goya with the savory flavors of other ingredients. The influence of Chinese and Southeast Asian cuisines is evident in dishes like rafute, braised pork belly that melts in your mouth.

At the bustling Makishi Public Market, you'll find a kaleidoscope of ingredients: vibrant sea grapes, pungent awamori liquor, and the dark, glistening kurozato sugar. The local food is a reflection of the islanders easy going nature, and long history. The fresh seafood is also a must try. Try 'Tsuboya Yatai' for a taste of local street food, or a restaurant that specializes in Okinawan cuisine for a full culinary experience.
